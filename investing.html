My Investing Philosophy

How I think about markets, risk, and long-term wealth building.

/ Philosophy

Think like an owner, not a trader.

At the core of my investing approach is a simple idea: when you buy a share of stock, you are buying a small ownership stake in a real business. That framing changes everything. Instead of obsessing over short-term price movements, I focus on understanding the underlying business — its revenue model, its competitive advantages, and the quality of its management team.

I'm heavily influenced by value investing principles. I look for businesses trading below their intrinsic value, with durable competitive moats — things like brand loyalty, network effects, switching costs, or cost advantages — that protect them from competition over time. Patience is essential: good businesses bought at fair prices tend to compound wealth steadily over years and decades.

I also pay close attention to macroeconomic factors. Interest rates, inflation expectations, and central bank policy all shape the environment in which businesses operate and how their future cash flows are valued today. Understanding the macro backdrop helps me size opportunities and manage risk more effectively.

Finally, I believe deeply in margin of safety — never paying so much for an asset that there's no cushion if things go wrong. Markets are unpredictable in the short run, and humility about what I don't know is just as important as confidence in what I do.

/ Core Principles

What I look for in an investment

  1. Durable competitive moat — Does the business have something that protects it from competition for years?
  2. Strong free cash flow generation — Can the company turn profits into actual cash reliably?
  3. Honest and capable management — Do the leaders allocate capital wisely and treat shareholders well?
  4. Reasonable valuation — Am I paying a fair or discounted price relative to intrinsic value?
  5. Long runway for growth — Is there a large, addressable market still ahead of the business?
  6. Margin of safety — Is there a buffer that protects me if my assumptions are wrong?
